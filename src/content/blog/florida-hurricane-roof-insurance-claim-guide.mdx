After a named storm, the speed and accuracy of how you file your roof insurance claim determines whether you walk out with a fully-funded replacement or a partial payment that leaves you covering tens of thousands out of pocket. Florida law gives carriers structured timelines they have to honor — and gives you obligations the carrier expects you to meet within those same windows.

This guide walks through every step of filing a Florida hurricane roof insurance claim in 2026 — from the pre-storm photo documentation that protects you, through the post-storm mitigation requirement, the claim filing itself, working with adjusters, appealing denials, and the realistic timeline to settlement. It is written from the contractor's side: the things we see go right when claims pay quickly, and the missteps that turn a 30-day claim into a 6-month battle.

Florida's claim landscape changed materially in 2022-2023. House Bill 837 shortened the time you have to file. Citizens Property Insurance — the state-backed insurer of last resort — has grown to become one of the largest carriers in the state, and its underwriting and claim handling differ from a private carrier. Older guides still floating around the internet reflect the pre-HB-837 rules and will lead you to miss filing deadlines.

If your roof was damaged by Hurricane Ian (Sep 2022), Nicole (Nov 2022), or Idalia (Aug 2023), the supplemental-claim window may still be open depending on your specific carrier — but the windows are tight. The fastest action you can take is starting your documentation today.

> **Beit Tip:** The single highest-leverage thing you can do RIGHT NOW — before any storm hits — is take dated photos of every slope of your roof, every flashing detail, and every penetration. Store them in cloud backup (Google Photos, iCloud) so the timestamps are server-verified. Your insurance carrier cannot deny pre-existing-damage claims for conditions that didn't exist in your timestamped photos.

## 1. Pre-storm prep: the photo documentation that protects you

The most common reason a Florida hurricane claim gets short-paid is the carrier alleging pre-existing damage. The fix is a complete pre-storm photo record dated to a time before the storm hit.

What to photograph annually (or after each major weather event):

- Every slope of your roof — full slope, ridge to eave, photographed from ground level with a telephoto lens or from a second-story window when possible
- Every flashing detail: chimney saddles, valley flashings, sidewall flashings, plumbing vent boots, skylights, satellite dish penetrations
- The eaves, fascia, and soffits — close-up so the paint condition and any rot is visible
- Gutters and downspouts
- The interior ceiling of every room, especially around skylights and adjacent to chimneys
- The attic — joists, decking, insulation — at the four corners and around any plumbing or electrical penetrations

Set the EXIF timestamp accuracy on your phone before you shoot. On iPhone: Settings → Camera → Preserve Settings, and confirm geo-tagging is enabled in Privacy → Location Services → Camera. Server-stamped photos in Google Photos and iCloud are even better, because the cloud's timestamp cannot be back-dated.

Update your homeowner insurance declarations page once a year and store it alongside the photos. Your agent can email you the current dec page on request — there is no charge.

> **Beit Tip:** Schedule your photo update for May. Florida's hurricane season starts June 1. If your photos are over a year old when a claim is filed, carriers will discount their evidentiary weight.

Keep copies of any roofing work you've had done — invoices, warranty documents, manufacturer registration cards, permit numbers. These prove the age of the roof and the materials installed, which directly impacts your claim's payout (replacement cost vs. depreciated actual cash value, covered in Section 8).

## 2. Immediate post-storm safety (the first 24-72 hours)

Once the storm passes:

**Power.** If lines are down or transformers are compromised, do not enter the home until utility crews have cleared it.

**Structural check.** From the OUTSIDE, walk the perimeter of the home. Look for: shifted roof lines, loose fascia, missing soffits, chimney displacement, displaced flashing on visible slopes. Do NOT climb onto the roof yourself. Active hurricane damage has hidden structural failures (sheathing nail withdrawal, broken trusses) that can collapse under your weight even if the surface looks intact.

**Water entry inside.** Walk every room. Look for ceiling stains of any size or location, water at electrical outlets, swollen door frames, bowing baseboards, damp drywall corners. Document with photo and video as you go — every observation, even small ones.

**Mitigation = your contractual obligation.** Every Florida homeowner insurance policy includes a duty to mitigate further damage. If water is actively entering, you must take reasonable steps to stop it. That typically means emergency tarping, which requires a licensed contractor — DIY tarping on a damaged roof is dangerous and rarely meets the carrier's documentation requirements.

> **Beit Tip:** If water is actively entering, call your carrier first, then call a licensed roofing contractor for emergency tarping. The emergency tarping receipt is mitigation proof and is reimbursed under the same claim.

What NOT to do in the first 72 hours:

- Don't sign anything an unsolicited contractor hands you. Storm chasers swarm Florida after every named storm. Section 10 covers this in detail.
- Don't move debris from the roof or yard until you've documented it (or the adjuster has).
- Don't pay for any repair beyond emergency tarping until the claim is approved and the scope of work is in writing.

## 3. Documenting damage (the make-or-break section)

The single most important section of this guide. The quality of your damage documentation determines what the carrier pays.

**Photo guide.** For every damaged area, capture three shots:

- **Wide shot:** full slope or full elevation showing the damaged area in context
- **Medium shot:** the damage area with a known scale reference (a 12-inch ruler, a tape measure, even a smartphone laid flat)
- **Close-up:** detail showing the failure mode (lifted shingle tab, broken tile, exposed underlayment, daylight visible through deck)
- **Below the damage on the interior:** any water staining, drywall failure, attic moisture

For each photo, capture: date (auto from EXIF), GPS location (auto if location services are enabled), and ideally a written caption (use a notes app and describe what you're shooting and why).

**Drone documentation.** A drone aerial of the full roof is gold-standard evidence for adjusters. If you don't own a drone, your contractor's documentation gives you the aerial perspective without the safety risk of climbing the roof. Beit drones every storm-damage claim by default — drone photos at 50-100 feet altitude show the damage in context that ground photos cannot.

**Attic moisture readings.** A pin-type moisture meter (under $50 at any home center) reads the water content of decking and structural framing. Wood at greater than 18% moisture content is at risk of mold and structural failure. Document readings in the four corners of the attic plus any visibly damaged area.

**Damage chronology.** Maintain a written timeline:

- Date and time the storm hit your address (NOAA's storm path data confirms this)
- When you first observed each damaged area
- When you took mitigation action (tarping, etc.)
- Every contact with the carrier — date, name of representative, claim or reference number

Save this in a single text file or notes app. When the claim gets disputed, the chronology proves you acted within the policy's time limits.

> **Beit Tip:** Time-stamp your damage photos AGAINST the storm. A photo taken 4 hours after a storm landed is harder for the carrier to dispute than one taken three weeks later.

## 4. Contacting your insurance carrier

Florida law under House Bill 837 (2023) requires you to file the notice of claim within ONE YEAR of the date of loss for hurricane claims — down from two years under the prior law. Supplemental and reopened claims must be filed within 18 months. Don't sit on the claim.

**When to call:** As soon as the property is safe to access AND you have at least preliminary photo documentation. That is typically 24-72 hours after the storm.

**What to say (the script that works):**

- "I'm calling to file a claim under policy [number]. There was hurricane damage at the property at [address] on [date]. I'd like a claim number and to schedule the carrier's adjuster inspection."
- "I have taken initial damage photographs and started mitigation per the policy's requirement. The contractor is [name] / I'm working on getting one."
- "What is the next step in my carrier's process? What documentation do you need from me?"
- Get the call reference number, the claim number, and the name plus direct contact for the assigned adjuster.

**What NOT to say:**

- Speculation about the cause ("I think this might be from before the storm" — never speculate against your own claim)
- Estimates of the dollar amount of damage (unless you have a written contractor estimate already in hand)
- Anything that suggests pre-existing damage ("the roof was getting old anyway")

**What to ask the carrier to provide:**

- The full policy declarations page (your "dec page")
- The current claims-handling timeline expectations
- Whether your policy is replacement cost (RCV) or actual cash value (ACV) — this is critical and detailed in Section 8
- Direct deposit information for claim payments
- The carrier's preferred contractor list, if any. You are NOT obligated to use it.

Florida Statute 627.70131 requires insurers to acknowledge receipt of your claim within 7 working days. If you don't get an acknowledgment in writing within 7 days, follow up — and document the follow-up in your chronology file.

## 5. Filing the claim

The claim form itself looks deceptively simple. Several specific fields are where homeowners trip up.

**The "scope of loss" statement.** This is your written description of what was damaged. Be specific, factual, and exhaustive:

- "Full architectural shingle roof, approximately 28 squares, complete failure with shingle uplift and underlayment exposure across all four slopes."
- "Interior water damage to ceiling drywall in master bedroom (8' x 12' affected area) and adjacent hallway, including insulation."
- "Three damaged plumbing vent boots; two displaced shingle ridge caps."

Avoid vague language like "lots of damage" — adjusters trained to minimize claims interpret vague language conservatively.

**Mitigation receipts.** Every dollar you spent on mitigation (emergency tarping, water extraction, temporary boarding, ventilation fans to prevent mold) is reimbursable under the same claim. Save every receipt — credit card statement copies work as proof if the original receipt is lost.

**Contractor documentation.** This is where having a licensed roofing contractor in early is most valuable. A licensed contractor's written scope of repair, materials list, and price quote is the gold standard for claim documentation. Carriers respect documentation prepared by a properly-licensed contractor more than homeowner-prepared estimates.

When choosing your contractor, verify their license is current and active before signing anything. Florida DBPR's public license search at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr) lets you paste any license number and see the live record. For a roofing claim, you want a Certified Roofing Contractor (CCC prefix). For interior structural damage from water intrusion, a Certified General Contractor (CGC prefix) license adds the structural authority.

## 6. Working with the adjuster

The carrier's adjuster will schedule an on-site inspection. This typically happens within 30 days of filing for major hurricane claims, faster for individual non-event claims.

**Appointment prep:**

- Have your photo documentation, chronology, and any contractor documentation in one folder
- Walk the property the morning of the appointment to refresh which damage you'll point out
- Have a paper copy of your policy declarations available
- Get your contractor on-site for the appointment if possible — adjusters and contractors talking directly resolves more questions than email back-and-forth

**What to walk them through:**

- Start at the most-damaged area and work systematically
- Point to specific damage, describe what failed and how
- Show the timestamped photos for any damage that's harder to see now (e.g., shingle pieces blown into the yard)
- Show the moisture readings in the attic
- Point out any interior damage room-by-room

**What to listen for:**

- Adjuster comments suggesting "wear and tear" or "pre-existing condition" (we cover the response in Section 8)
- Suggestions to "skip" certain damage — the adjuster might be testing whether you'll under-document
- Questions about the age of the roof or specific materials (have your records ready)

**After the adjuster leaves:**

- Document what was discussed, what they took photos of, and any specific points raised
- Request a written copy of their inspection report — Florida law says you're entitled to it
- Don't accept any verbal "settlement number" — get every figure in writing

If the adjuster's findings differ materially from your contractor's scope, you (or your contractor) can request a re-inspection or invoke the appraisal clause in your policy. We cover this in Section 8.

## 8. Common denial reasons + how to appeal

The four most common denial reasons we see in Florida hurricane claims:

**1. Pre-existing damage.** The carrier alleges the damage existed before the storm. Defense: timestamped pre-storm photos. Section 1 covers the documentation that prevents this.

**2. "Wear and tear" exclusion.** Carriers argue some damage is from age, not the storm. Defense: a contractor's report distinguishing storm-cause damage (impact, lift, displacement) from age-cause (granule loss, blistering). Florida Statute 627.7011 requires carriers to pay for storm damage even if the roof was old — depreciation may apply, but they cannot refuse the claim entirely on age grounds.

**3. Replacement Cost Value (RCV) vs. Actual Cash Value (ACV).** This is critical. ACV pays the depreciated value (a 15-year-old roof gets a fraction of replacement cost). RCV pays the full replacement cost. Read your dec page — if your policy is RCV, the carrier owes the full replacement amount minus your deductible, NOT the depreciated value.

**4. Mitigation deficiency.** Carriers argue you didn't take reasonable steps to prevent further damage. Defense: emergency tarping receipt, dated photos showing the property was secured, your written chronology.

**Appeal options if the claim is denied or short-paid:**

- **Re-inspection request.** Submit your contractor's documentation in writing and request a second adjuster visit.
- **Appraisal clause.** Most Florida policies include an "appraisal" clause: each side picks an appraiser, the two appraisers pick an umpire, and the three resolve disputed amounts. This is faster and cheaper than litigation.
- **Department of Insurance complaint.** Florida's Office of Insurance Regulation (OIR) accepts consumer complaints and reviews carrier handling. File at floir.com.
- **Public adjuster.** Licensed independently to negotiate claims on your behalf, typically for a percentage of recovery.
- **Litigation.** Last resort — Florida's 2022 reforms (Senate Bill 2A) tightened the timelines and requirements for litigating insurance claims, so this path is harder than it was previously.

## 9. Realistic timeline

What to expect, working forward from the storm:

- **Days 1-3:** Mitigation, initial documentation, file notice of claim
- **Day 7:** Carrier acknowledgment per F.S. 627.70131 (in writing)
- **Days 14-30:** Adjuster on-site inspection
- **Days 30-60:** Initial determination from carrier (approval, partial approval, or denial)
- **Days 60-90:** Final determination per F.S. 627.70131 deadline (typically 90 days from receipt of complete claim, with documented exceptions)
- **Days 90+:** Repair scheduling — the licensed contractor backlog after a major storm is typically 60-180 days for non-emergency permanent repairs

The 90-day clock pauses if the carrier requests additional documentation. Stay responsive — every delay on your side resets that clock.

## 10. Scams and red flags

After every Florida named storm, fraud follows the storm chasers. Watch for:

**Door-knocking contractors.** Out-of-state contractors who appear after a storm and disappear before warranties come due. Verify any contractor's license at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr) before signing anything. Florida law requires roofing contractors to hold a Certified Roofing Contractor (CCC) license OR a Registered Roofing Contractor (RC) license — anyone telling you otherwise is unlicensed.

**Assignment of Benefits (AOB) abuse.** AOBs let a contractor stand in your shoes for the claim. After Florida's 2022 reforms, AOBs are heavily restricted for property-insurance claims but not eliminated. Read every form before signing — if a contractor pushes you to sign an AOB on the first visit, walk away.

**"Free roof" pitches.** A contractor offering a free roof in exchange for "filing the claim for you" is almost always a scam or fraud. Insurance fraud is a felony in Florida.

**Pressure to sign immediately.** Legitimate contractors give you time to read the contract and verify the license. Anyone pressuring "sign today or the storm-damage discount expires" is signaling a problem.

**Cash-only deals.** Reputable contractors accept multiple payment methods and provide written invoices. Cash-only is a tax-evasion or unlicensed signal.

## 11. Quick FAQ

### How long do I have to file a Florida hurricane roof claim?

One year from the date of loss under House Bill 837 (2023). Supplemental claims have an 18-month window.

### Does my mortgage company need to be involved?

For larger claim payouts, yes — most policies require the mortgagee on the check. Your carrier will handle the routing if you provide your mortgage company's contact info.

### Can I use any contractor I want?

Yes. Carriers may suggest a "preferred" contractor list, but you're not obligated to use it. Use any licensed Florida contractor.

### What if my deductible is huge?

Florida hurricane deductibles are typically 2-5% of the dwelling coverage (so a $500,000 home might have a $25,000 hurricane deductible). The carrier cannot waive it. Some policies allow you to "buy down" to a lower deductible — check your dec page.

### Do I have to pay the contractor before insurance pays?

Most Florida contractors accept the insurance proceeds directly via check made co-payable to you and the contractor. You typically pay the deductible up front and your carrier covers the rest.

### What if the carrier delays past 90 days?

Florida's prompt-pay rules give you escalation paths. If the carrier exceeds the deadline without documented reason, file an OIR complaint at floir.com.
