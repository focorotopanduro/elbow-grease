The most expensive way to replace a roof is one repair at a time. Florida homeowners overspend on partial fixes every year — small repairs that add up to more than a full replacement would have cost — because nobody clearly explained the inflection point.

This guide is the framework we walk every Beit Building Contractors customer through when they call asking "do I need a new roof, or just a repair?" The honest answer depends on five signals, the age and material of the existing roof, and what your insurance policy actually covers — including provisions in F.S. 627.7011 that most homeowners never read.

> **Beit Tip:** When in doubt, get **two written opinions from separately-licensed contractors**. Both should be Certified Roofing Contractors (CCC prefix on Florida DBPR). Verify both at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr) before accepting either recommendation. The cost differential between repair and replacement at the inflection point is often $5,000-$25,000 — well worth a second free inspection to settle the question.

## 5 signs your Florida roof needs replacement (not just repair)

Any one of these alone is a strong signal. Two or more, and the math almost always favors full replacement.

### 1. Age plus material exceeds expected Florida lifespan

Florida's UV + thermal cycling + hurricane wind compresses every roofing material's lifespan compared to manufacturer ratings (which are typically based on Mid-Atlantic climate). See our [Florida Roofing Materials Guide](/blog/florida-roofing-materials-guide-2026) for full lifespan-by-material ranges. As a quick reference: 3-tab asphalt past 15 years, architectural shingle past 20 years, concrete tile past 40 years, metal past 50 years — replacement is usually the right call regardless of visible damage.

### 2. Multiple slope failures simultaneously

A single damaged slope from a known event (a tree limb, a localized hail strike) is repairable. **Multiple slopes showing wear, lift, or granule loss simultaneously** signals the entire roof system is at end-of-life — not that several independent repairs are needed.

### 3. Structural deck damage, sagging, or soft spots

When the contractor walks the roof and reports soft spots underfoot, visible sagging between trusses, or daylight visible through decking from the attic — the roof's structural foundation has compromised. Patching the surface over a soft deck doesn't fix it; it hides it for one or two more storms.

### 4. Insurance carrier non-renewal threat or steep premium increases

Many Florida carriers (Citizens Property Insurance, State Farm, Progressive, others) decline to renew policies on roofs older than 25 years — sometimes 15 years for 3-tab shingle. If your renewal letter mentions the roof age or premium has jumped substantially, the carrier is signaling that replacement is necessary to stay insured at reasonable rates.

### 5. Granule loss approaching bare felt — or repeated tile cracking from thermal cycling

For shingles: when granule loss exposes the underlying asphalt across multiple slopes, UV degradation accelerates rapidly. The roof has months, not years, before active leaks. For tile: repeated cracking driven by Florida's daily 60°F+ thermal cycling between night and afternoon attic temperatures means the tile bodies have lost their elasticity.

## 5 signs repair is enough (don't pay for replacement yet)

### 1. Damage is localized to a single slope or quadrant

A storm tore three shingles off the south slope. A tree limb dented the east-facing valley. Lightning fractured a single tile. **Localized damage with otherwise-sound surrounding material is a textbook repair scenario** — and trying to push a homeowner toward full replacement on a localized event is a red flag for the contractor's integrity.

### 2. Underlying decking is sound

The contractor walks the roof, lifts a few shingles or tiles around the damaged area, inspects the deck below: no soft spots, no rot, no sheathing nail withdrawal. Repair the surface; the rest of the system has years of life remaining.

### 3. The roof is still under manufacturer warranty

Most architectural shingles carry 25-50 year warranties; most metal carries 30-100 years; tile is often lifetime. If the warranty is active AND covers the failure mode (most exclude wind damage past Class F rating in their standard terms — read the warranty language), pursue the warranty claim before replacement.

### 4. Less than half the roof's expected lifespan is used up

A 7-year-old architectural shingle roof in good general condition with one localized damaged area is a repair candidate. Same roof at 18 years old is a replacement candidate, even if the damage is localized — the rest of the roof is approaching end-of-life, and patching extends the lifespan by months, not years.

### 5. The repair doesn't recur

If the same flashing fails twice within 24 months, **the third repair is throwing money away** — the underlying installation or design has a defect that isolated patching won't fix. At that point, partial or full replacement is the economic choice.

> **Beit Tip:** **Never repair the same area twice.** Two repairs in the same location signal a deeper system failure — typically underlayment age or improper original installation. The third "repair" should be a partial or full replacement, not another patch.

## Florida age thresholds by material (the lifespan-discount math)

Manufacturer-published lifespans are based on national-average climate. Florida's UV + thermal cycling + hurricane exposure compresses these by roughly 25-35%. Realistic Central Florida service life:

| Material | Manufacturer rating | Realistic Central FL service life |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3-tab asphalt shingle | 20-25 years | 12-18 years |
| Architectural (laminate) shingle | 25-50 years | 18-25 years |
| Class 4 impact-resistant shingle (UL 2218) | 30-50 years | 22-30 years (algae-resistant lines extend further) |
| Concrete tile | 50 years | 40-50 years |
| Clay tile | 75+ years | 50-75+ years (many original 1920s tile roofs in Park Avenue district still on the home) |
| Exposed-fastener metal (5V crimp, R-panel) | 40-50 years | 30-50 years (fastener gaskets need re-tightening every 8-12 years) |
| Standing-seam metal | 50-100 years | 50-75+ years |
| TPO / EPDM membrane (low-slope) | 20-30 years | 15-25 years |

Use the lower end of each range for **roofs that have endured** at least one Category-3-or-greater hurricane (Ian, Nicole, Charley, etc.) without a documented post-storm inspection. Storms accelerate aging measurably even when no immediate damage was visible.

> **Beit Tip:** A roof's age is the age of the **shingles or tiles** plus the age of the **underlayment beneath them**. If the surface was replaced 8 years ago but the underlayment dates to the original 1995 build, the roof system effectively reads as 30 years old to a thoughtful inspector — which means closer to replacement than a homeowner might assume.

## The hidden costs of repeated repairs

Repair feels cheap individually. Three repairs over six years often costs more than a full replacement, with worse outcomes. The hidden math:

- **Truck-roll minimums.** Every Florida contractor visit includes labor, dump fees, and basic supplies — typically $350-600 minimum charge before the first nail is driven, regardless of how small the actual repair scope is.
- **Material patch-matching gets harder.** Shingle color lots cycle every 5-10 years. After a decade, your contractor is matching against close approximations rather than identical product. Visible color mismatch hurts curb appeal and resale.
- **Underlayment age compounds.** Replacing surface materials over an aged underlayment doesn't address the underlayment's failure point — you're paying to delay an inevitable full re-roof by 1-3 years.
- **Decking deterioration.** Spot repairs that don't address marginal decking are cosmetic. The next failure happens through the same compromised deck panel.
- **Insurance premium acceleration.** Florida insurers re-evaluate premiums based on roof age annually. Each year above ~15 years of roof age compounds premium increases — sometimes faster than the savings from delaying replacement.

Typical Central Florida 2026 cost ranges:

- **Spot repair (single slope, 1-3 shingles or tiles):** $400-1,500
- **Partial replacement (one slope, ~1/4 of total roof):** $2,000-6,000
- **Full replacement (architectural shingle, average residential 18-25 squares):** $8,000-15,000
- **Full replacement (concrete tile, same square footage):** $14,000-25,000
- **Full replacement (standing-seam metal):** $20,000-35,000+

These are typical ranges, not quotes. Actual pricing varies with your specific square footage, decking condition, FBC §1518 SWB requirement, and material selection (see our [materials guide](/blog/florida-roofing-materials-guide-2026) for the full breakdown).

## The insurance angle (F.S. 627.7011 and what it means for you)

Florida Statute 627.7011 governs homeowner property-loss valuation and gives insured Floridians important protections most never read.

**Replacement Cost Value (RCV) vs Actual Cash Value (ACV).** Read your declarations page carefully — it specifies which form of valuation applies:

- **RCV:** the carrier owes the cost to replace the damaged property with new materials of like kind and quality, minus your deductible. No depreciation deduction.
- **ACV:** the carrier owes the depreciated value (a 20-year-old roof gets a fraction of replacement cost). The homeowner makes up the difference out of pocket.

If your policy is RCV (most newer Florida policies are), and your roof has reached the inflection point where repeated repairs are uneconomic, **the carrier may owe the full replacement value** under the policy — even if individual storm-damage events were partial. This is especially true after hurricane events where multiple repair claims have accumulated.

**Repair-uneconomic threshold.** When a contractor's documented repair quote approaches a substantial percentage of replacement cost (carriers vary; some use 50-60% as the trigger), policies typically cover full replacement under "law and ordinance" coverage and replacement-cost provisions. Get this in writing during the claim documentation phase — see our [Hurricane Roof Insurance Claim Guide](/blog/florida-hurricane-roof-insurance-claim-guide) for the full claim playbook.

**Carrier non-renewal context.** Many Florida carriers explicitly require roof replacement (or refuse renewal) at 25 years for shingle, sometimes 15-20 for older 3-tab, regardless of visible condition. If your renewal letter has mentioned roof age, the conversation has shifted: replacement may be required to remain insured at all, regardless of what visible damage exists.

> **Beit Tip:** Before assuming a major repair is your responsibility out-of-pocket, **read the dec page** and confirm RCV vs ACV. Then call a licensed contractor for a written assessment that documents whether repair is uneconomic. If it is, file the insurance claim with both documents in hand.

## Decision framework (the simple flow)

1. **Is damage localized AND decking + underlayment sound AND less than 60% of expected lifespan used?** → Repair.
2. **Is damage localized AND lifespan threshold approaching (60-80% used)?** → Repair, with a 12-month re-inspection on the calendar AND a budget line for replacement within 24-36 months.
3. **Is age past 80% of expected lifespan, OR insurance non-renewal looming, OR multiple slope failures, OR structural deck damage?** → Replace.
4. **Anywhere in between, or carrier and contractor disagree?** → Get a second written opinion from a separately-licensed CCC contractor.
