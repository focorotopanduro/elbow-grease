After every named storm in Florida, fraud follows the storm chasers. After every season, Florida homeowners file complaints with the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) about contractors who took deposits and never came back, did unpermitted work, or pressured customers into Assignment of Benefits forms that signed away their claim rights. Most of these problems are preventable in the **5 minutes** it takes to verify the contractor before signing anything.

This guide walks through that 5-minute due-diligence process step-by-step. The most important step — the DBPR live license search — costs nothing, takes under a minute, and is the single most reliable signal of contractor legitimacy in Florida.

> **Beit Tip:** Verify EVERY contractor you consider, even ones recommended by neighbors or insurance carriers. A 2-minute DBPR check is the only proof of legitimate licensing — verbal claims, business cards, and even truck-decal license numbers can be expired or impersonated.

## 1. The DBPR live license search (the most important step)

Florida's Department of Business and Professional Regulation maintains a free public database of every licensed contractor in the state. Search any license number or contractor name; see the live record.

**URL:** [myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr)

**Step-by-step:**

1. Open the link above. The page is the DBPR's "Public License Search" — title bar reads "Department of Business and Professional Regulation - License Search."
2. The form defaults to **Search by License Number**. If a contractor gave you a license number, paste it into the **License Number** field and click **Search**. (You can also search by Name or by County.)
3. Results show: license type, status, expiration date, qualifier name (the licensed individual responsible), and the DBA (organization name).

**What you want to see:**

- Status: **Current, Active**
- Expiration: in the future, ideally with a year or more remaining
- Qualifier name: matches the contractor company's representative who answered your call
- DBA: matches the company name on their proposals + invoices

**What's a problem:**

- Status: **Expired**, **Voluntarily Inactive**, **Suspended**, or **Revoked** — walk away
- License doesn't exist in the DBPR database — walk away (the contractor is unlicensed)
- DBA differs from the company name they presented to you — they may be a sales-only operation reselling work to a different licensed entity (legal but worth understanding before signing)

## 2. License types — what CCC, CGC, RC, and RR actually authorize

Florida Statute Chapter 489 (Construction Industry Licensing Board) defines distinct license categories, each authorizing different scopes of work.

### Certified vs Registered

- **Certified (C-prefix)** — state-wide authority. The contractor can pull permits and perform work anywhere in Florida.
- **Registered (R-prefix)** — local authority only. The contractor can only work in the specific county or municipality where the license is registered. Registered contractors typically have less rigorous testing requirements than certified.

For a Florida homeowner, **certified is the safer choice** — you don't have to worry about jurisdictional limits, and the higher testing bar means deeper code knowledge.

### License-letter codes (the part that matters most)

- **CCC** — Certified Roofing Contractor. State-wide. Authorizes all roofing work — replacement, repair, materials.
- **CGC** — Certified General Contractor. State-wide. Authorizes whole-structure construction, additions, and renovation projects up to and including new builds. A CGC can perform structural framing, decking, and exterior systems.
- **CBC** — Certified Building Contractor. Limited to commercial structures up to three stories.
- **CRC** — Certified Residential Contractor. Limited to residential up to two stories.
- **RC** — Registered Roofing Contractor. County-level authority for roofing.
- **RR** — Older roofing registration code, less common today.

For a roofing project, look for a **CCC**. For interior structural damage from water intrusion (storm-damage scope or a major renovation), you'll also want **CGC** authority — without it, the contractor has to subcontract framing/structural work out, which adds cost and creates accountability gaps.

## 3. Insurance verification (the COI you need to see)

Florida licensing requires roofing contractors to carry Workers' Compensation coverage and General Liability insurance. **Always ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) before signing.**

A legitimate COI:

- Is issued by the contractor's insurance carrier directly (you can request it from the carrier's portal — easy to fake one as a PDF, harder to fake one delivered through ACORD or carrier email)
- Lists **you, the homeowner, as Certificate Holder** — this is critical; the COI must name you specifically
- Shows current effective dates (not expired)
- Lists General Liability coverage limits (typical: $1M per occurrence, $2M aggregate)
- Lists Workers' Compensation (mandatory in Florida for any contractor with employees)

Workers' Comp matters more than homeowners realize: if a worker is injured on your property and the contractor lacks coverage, **the injured worker can sue the homeowner directly**. Don't skip this verification.

## 4. Reputation checks (and the ones that don't matter)

Reviews are signal — but not all signals are equal.

**High-value reputation checks:**

- **Google Business Profile reviews.** Volume + recency + sentiment. Look for: 25+ reviews, recent activity (last 6 months), patterns in 4-5 star reviews mentioning specific job details (not generic "great service" comments).
- **Better Business Bureau profile.** BBB tracks complaints + how the contractor responded. Pay attention to patterns: did the contractor reply, take the issue seriously, resolve it? Or did they ignore complaints?
- **Real neighborhood references** — ask the contractor for two or three customer addresses on streets adjacent to yours. Drive by; talk to the homeowners. This is by far the most reliable signal for a local contractor.

**Lower-value or noisy signals:**

- **Yelp** — Yelp's review filter aggressively hides newer reviews from infrequent reviewers, which means a small contractor's Yelp profile can be misleadingly thin.
- **Sponsored "best of" lists** — many are pay-to-play. The contractor pays the publisher to be listed.
- **Generic "5-star company" verbal claims** — anyone can claim a star rating; ask to see the specific review surface.

For making the actual decision between two contractors, the [Repair vs Replace decision framework](/blog/repair-vs-replace-roof-florida) we walk through is more reliable than either contractor's review profile alone.

## 5. Red flags — walk away when you see these

After every named Florida storm, fraud follows. The pattern is consistent enough to memorize:

**Door-knocking after a storm.** Out-of-state contractors arrive with branded vehicles, claim to have inspected your roof "from the road," and pressure you to sign on the spot. Florida law (F.S. Chapter 489) requires roofing contractors to hold a Florida license — verify on the DBPR portal **before** any conversation about signing.

**Pressure to sign Assignment of Benefits (AOB) forms** at the first visit. AOBs let the contractor stand in your shoes for the insurance claim. After Florida's 2021 Senate Bill 76 and the 2022 reforms (F.S. 627.7152), AOBs are heavily restricted for property-insurance claims, but bad actors still push them. Read every form before signing — if you're being pressured at the first visit, walk away. See the scams section of our [Hurricane Roof Insurance Claim Guide](/blog/florida-hurricane-roof-insurance-claim-guide) for full context.

**"Free roof" pitches.** A contractor offering a free roof in exchange for "filing the claim for you" or "letting them handle the deductible" is almost always insurance fraud. Florida treats insurance fraud as a felony.

**Cash-only deals.** Reputable contractors accept multiple payment methods and provide written invoices. Cash-only is a tax-evasion or unlicensed signal.

**No-permit promises.** Any roof replacement in Florida requires a permit pulled with the local building department under FBC 7th Edition (2024) requirements. A contractor offering to skip the permit to "save money" is exposing you (the property owner) to code violations and an unsellable home.

**Unusually low quotes.** When a quote comes in 30-50% below the others for the same scope, the difference is almost always one of: unlicensed labor, no insurance, no permit, no FBC §1518 secondary water barrier, or a plan to escalate scope mid-project after work begins.

**No physical address or website.** Florida licensing requires a physical business address. A contractor who only gives you a P.O. Box, a cell number, and a Facebook page is signaling they don't have the infrastructure to honor a multi-year warranty.

> **Beit Tip:** **Never give a deposit larger than 10% to a contractor you've just met.** Florida law allows up to 10% as a deposit for a residential project, but reputable contractors typically structure payment as deposit-at-signing, progress-at-midpoint, balance-at-completion. A contractor demanding 50% up front is a bad sign.

## 6. The contract review (what to look for before signing)

Read every page. Specifically check for:

- **Itemized scope of work** — line items, not lumped totals. Should call out: tear-off, FBC §1518 SWB, decking allowance, flashing, ventilation, disposal, permit fees.
- **Material specifications** — brand and product line by name (e.g., "GAF Timberline HDZ in Charcoal" or "concrete tile, Eagle Roofing Bel Air profile"). Generic "asphalt shingle, color TBD" is a contract escape hatch.
- **Timeline** — written start date and completion target. Reasonable weather contingencies are fine; open-ended "we'll start when we get to it" is not.
- **Warranty terms** — manufacturer warranty (which one, what's covered, transferable to next owner?) AND contractor's workmanship warranty (typical: 5-10 years, what's covered, what voids it).
- **Payment schedule** — deposit at signing, progress at substantial milestone (typically tear-off complete or decking inspected), balance at final walkthrough. AVOID schedules that require full payment before completion.
- **Permit responsibility** — the contractor pulls and closes the permit. Get the permit number documented in the contract.
- **Mitigation in event of mid-project delays** — if the project is 2 days into a 5-day re-roof and weather hits, who's responsible for emergency tarping?
- **Change-order policy** — written change orders are mandatory. No verbal "we found something extra, add $4,000" mid-project.

For a typical re-roof, the contract should be 4-8 pages. Anything shorter is incomplete; anything longer than 12 pages is being padded. Read every page anyway — the gotchas are usually in the back.

## 7. The 5-minute due-diligence checklist (save this list)

Print or screenshot this list. Use it for every contractor you evaluate.

1. **License verified at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp?mode=1&search=LicNbr)** — current, active, qualifier name matches representative
2. **License type appropriate** — CCC for roofing; CGC if structural/general scope is involved
3. **COI received and verifies** — General Liability $1M+, Workers' Comp current, names you as Certificate Holder
4. **Physical address listed** — not just a P.O. Box or cell number
5. **Reviews check out** — 25+ Google reviews, BBB profile shows responsive complaint handling, real neighborhood references provided
6. **No red flags** — no door-knock pressure, no AOB ask at first visit, not cash-only, permit included
7. **Written itemized contract** — scope, materials by brand, timeline, warranty (manufacturer + workmanship), payment schedule, permit number, change-order policy
8. **Deposit is 10% or less**

Any one item failing is a yellow flag. Two or more is a red flag. All eight passing means you can sign with reasonable confidence.
