Florida's Atlantic hurricane season runs from June 1 through November 30. The first named storm typically forms in early-to-mid June; the peak — when the Atlantic basin produces the strongest, most damaging storms — is mid-August through late October. Orange County and most of Central Florida sit in the ASCE 7-22 wind-design zone for 130 mph 3-second gusts; Hurricane Ian (Sep 2022) and Hurricane Nicole (Nov 2022) both caused measurable wind and water damage to thousands of Central Florida roofs within seven weeks of each other.

This is the checklist we walk every Beit Building Contractors customer through before each season. It's organized into three time windows — **30 days before**, **7 days before**, and **24 hours before** — plus the year-round practices that compound over time. Print it, refresh it every May, and the thirty minutes you invest annually pays off across every claim that ever hits your policy.

> **Beit Tip:** The single most important item on this list is the **annual May photo refresh** of every roof slope, flashing, and gutter. Server-stamped cloud photos (Google Photos, iCloud) are what protect your claim from the most common denial reason — alleged pre-existing damage. Skip everything else if you have to, but never skip the photos.

## Why this checklist matters

Every Florida homeowner insurance policy includes a **mitigation clause** — you are contractually obligated to take reasonable steps to prevent further damage to your property, both before and during a storm. Carriers can deny claims when the homeowner failed to mitigate. House Bill 837 (2023) shortened the window to file a hurricane claim to **one year from the date of loss**; supplemental claims have an 18-month window. Storm damage you don't document and file in time is storm damage you pay for yourself.

The compounding benefit: pre-storm photo documentation is what protects your claim from the most common denial reason — pre-existing damage. Your insurance carrier cannot reasonably deny pre-existing-damage claims for conditions that didn't exist in your timestamped photos. The full claim playbook is in our [Hurricane Roof Insurance Claim Guide](/blog/florida-hurricane-roof-insurance-claim-guide); this post focuses on what you do before the storm hits, so the playbook works.

## 30 days before: foundation prep (do this every May)

Block off two hours on the first weekend of May. The list:

- **Schedule a free roof inspection** with a licensed Florida contractor. We offer them across [Orlando](/orlando-roofing), [Winter Park](/winter-park-roofing), and [Oviedo](/oviedo-roofing) — no obligation, written report within 48 hours, includes both ground-walk and (when conditions permit) drone aerial documentation.
- **Take dated photos** of every roof slope from ground level, every flashing detail, every penetration. Walk every interior room and shoot the ceilings. Document the attic in all four corners. Save in cloud-backup so timestamps are server-verified, not phone-clock-editable.
- **Review your insurance declarations page.** Confirm: deductibles (especially the separate hurricane deductible — typically 2-5% of dwelling coverage), coverage limits, replacement-cost-value (RCV) vs actual-cash-value (ACV) status. Email your agent for the current dec page if you don't have it on hand.
- **Trim tree limbs within 10 feet of the roof.** Limbs are the leading cause of impact damage during named storms. Trim by mid-May so cut wounds have time to seal before peak season.
- **Clean gutters + downspouts.** Clogged gutters back water under the roofing edge — that's how most "interior leak" tickets actually start, before any wind even hits.
- **Verify your secondary water barrier (FBC §1518).** Florida Building Code §1518 mandates a self-adhered SBS underlayment under your roofing material in HVHZ counties and is strongly recommended throughout Florida. If your roof was installed before 2008 and hasn't been re-roofed since, you likely don't have one. Adding an SWB at your next re-roof costs $0.50-1.50/sq ft and is the single most impactful storm-readiness upgrade short of a full roof replacement.
- **Check the [My Safe Florida Home](https://mysafeflhome.com/) program.** State-funded grants of up to $10,000 (with $2 of grant for every $1 of homeowner contribution) for hurricane mitigation upgrades — including roof-deck attachment improvements, SWB installation, and gable-end bracing. Application opens annually; check eligibility for the current year.

> **Beit Tip:** Schedule your free inspection in **April or early May**, not late May. Reputable contractors book up fast as season approaches, and any minor repairs the inspection reveals need time to be permitted, scheduled, and completed before June 1.

## 7 days before a forecasted storm

When a named storm enters the 7-day cone:

- **Watch NOAA's National Hurricane Center.** [nhc.noaa.gov](https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/) is the only forecast source you need. Local TV graphics get clicks; NOAA gets the science right.
- **Stock supplies.** Water (one gallon per person per day, 7-day minimum), non-perishable food, batteries, flashlights, a manual can opener, prescriptions for two weeks, generator fuel, fully-charged power banks.
- **Know your evacuation zone.** Orange County's zones are A, B, C, D, E. Look yours up at [ocfl.net](https://www.ocfl.net/EmergencySafety/EmergencyManagement.aspx). Seminole County uses similar lettered zones — verify at [seminolecountyfl.gov](https://www.seminolecountyfl.gov/).
- **Stage emergency tarping gear** OR have your contractor's number in your phone. If you DIY tarp, stock 16x20 ft heavy-duty blue tarps, 1×4 lumber strips, and 2-inch screws. **DIY tarping is dangerous on a damaged roof and rarely meets carrier documentation requirements** — call us at (407) 942-6459 for [emergency tarping in Orange or Seminole counties](/oviedo-storm-damage).
- **Photograph any new damage** from afternoon thunderstorms during the watch window. The more frequently you re-document, the harder it is for an adjuster to argue damage was pre-existing.
- **Update your damage chronology file.** Maintain a single text file or notes app listing every weather event affecting the property + every contact with your carrier. This file is what proves you acted within the policy's time limits when claims get disputed.
- **Confirm your contractor's number is saved.** During and immediately after the storm, every Florida roofing crew is overwhelmed. The contractors who answer first respond first.

## 24 hours before landfall

Everything in this window is fast and decisive:

- **Final exterior photo walk.** Five minutes. Phone in hand. Every slope. Every flashing. Every penetration. The interior ceilings of every room. Save to cloud immediately so the timestamps land in iCloud/Google before signal degrades.
- **Bring loose objects inside.** Patio furniture, gas grills, garbage cans, planters, garden tools, kids' toys. Anything that can become a 90-mph projectile when wind hits.
- **Stake or trim any new tree growth** within striking distance of the roof. The 30-day-before pass should have caught most of this; this is the cleanup pass.
- **Charge devices, fill prescriptions, fill water containers.** Bathtubs filled with water serve for sanitation if water service goes out. Power banks, laptops, phones, backup phones — all to 100%.
- **Move valuables to interior rooms** away from windows. Important documents (insurance dec page, property deed, recent photos of valuables for claim documentation) into a waterproof container.
- **Document chain-of-custody** on the field notes. Sign and date the chronology file. If you have to evacuate, take the file with you — physical or digital.

> **Beit Tip:** Photograph your **vehicles + license plates + VINs** in the same 24-hour window. Auto policies have similar mitigation clauses and a photo of an undamaged vehicle hours before the storm is just as protective as a roof photo.

## After the storm passes

The 24-72 hours after a storm passes are detailed in our [full Hurricane Roof Insurance Claim Guide](/blog/florida-hurricane-roof-insurance-claim-guide) — including post-storm safety, structural inspection from the outside, when to call the carrier, what to say (and not say) on that call, and how to document damage so claims pay quickly.

The high-level sequence:

1. **Wait until utility crews clear** if power lines are down or transformers are compromised
2. **Walk the property perimeter from outside** — never climb a damaged roof
3. **Document damage immediately** with timestamped photos, drone aerial if safe to fly
4. **Call your carrier within 24-72 hours** and file the notice of claim
5. **Mitigate further damage** — emergency tarping if water is actively entering. We dispatch within 4-6 hours of call across Orange and Seminole counties for [Oviedo-area storm response](/oviedo-storm-damage).
6. **Document everything in the chronology file** — every observation, every contact, every action

## Year-round prep that compounds

The habits that pay off across every season:

- **Annual May photo refresh** — already covered above. The single highest-leverage habit.
- **FBC §1518 secondary water barrier** retrofit at your next re-roof. If you have a 15+ year old roof that's nearing replacement, plan the SWB into the scope. Adds modest cost; massively reduces interior damage when the roofing material itself fails.
- **My Safe Florida Home grant application** — re-apply each year if eligible. Funding cycles and program rules change annually; the program was reauthorized in 2022 and continues to fund mitigation upgrades through the early 2030s.
- **Class 4 impact-resistant shingles** at re-roof. UL 2218 Class 4 shingles often qualify for a 5-15% insurance premium discount with most Florida carriers — over the shingle's lifetime, the discount typically more than offsets the upgrade cost.
- **Standing-seam metal upgrade** for premium hurricane resilience. The best-performing residential roof system in 150+ mph events when installed properly. Higher upfront cost, 50-70+ year lifespan. Worth considering for older homes where the next re-roof is the last re-roof you'll personally fund.

> **Beit Tip:** When a contractor quotes you a re-roof, ask explicitly: **"Is the FBC §1518 secondary water barrier included? Can you install Class 4 impact-resistant shingles? What's the wind-class rating?"** A contractor who doesn't have ready answers to these three questions is not the contractor for your hurricane-zone home.
