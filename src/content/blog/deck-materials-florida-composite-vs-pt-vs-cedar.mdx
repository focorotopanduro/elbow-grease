Picking a deck material in Florida is not the same decision homeowners face anywhere else in the country. Sustained 80%+ summer humidity drives moisture cycling that warps and rots untreated wood. UV bleaches surface finishes 2x faster than in northern states. Subterranean and Formosan termites — Florida is the highest-pressure termite state in the US — eat through anything organic that isn't properly treated. And pool chemicals (chlorine, salt-water systems) accelerate finish degradation on the most-used decks of all.

This guide compares the four realistic deck-material categories for Central Florida — pressure-treated pine, composite, cedar, and tropical hardwoods — with honest 2026 cost ranges, lifespan expectations, and the decision framework we use when a homeowner asks "which one should I pick?" Plus the aluminum option specifically for pool decks.

> **Beit Tip:** No single deck material is "best" for every Florida home. The right answer depends on your priority — lowest upfront cost, lowest maintenance, premium look, or longest lifespan. The decision matrix at the bottom of this post sorts the trade-offs.

## The Florida-climate problem

Five climate factors compound on every Florida deck:

1. **Humidity (80%+ summer averages).** Wood absorbs and releases moisture continuously, expanding and contracting with the daily cycle. Materials that don't tolerate this cycle warp, check, and split.
2. **UV exposure (230+ sunny days/year).** Surface finishes degrade fast. A finish that lasts 4 years in Vermont may last 18-24 months in Orlando.
3. **Termite pressure.** Subterranean termites are everywhere; Formosan termites are aggressive on the Gulf and East coasts of Florida. Untreated wood directly on or near grade is at high risk.
4. **Pool chemistry.** Chlorine, salt, and bromine accelerate corrosion of metal fasteners and degrade surface finishes faster than freshwater exposure.
5. **Hurricane wind + occasional flooding.** Decks attached to homes face the same wind-uplift loads as roofs (FBC R507 governs framing + ledger attachment); flooding from named storms saturates organic materials repeatedly.

Each material category responds differently. None is immune.

## Pressure-treated pine (the entry-level standard)

Southern Yellow Pine is the dominant Florida lumber for decking — it's structurally strong, locally produced, and accepts pressure-treatment chemicals well.

**Treatment chemistries.** Modern PT lumber uses ACQ (Alkaline Copper Quaternary), CA-B/CA-C (Copper Azole), or MCA (Micronized Copper Azole). All three handle Florida ground contact when correctly rated.

**Use-category rating.** This is the part homeowners almost always miss — and contractors should always verify. PT lumber comes tagged with use categories:

- **UC3B** — above-ground exterior, dries quickly. Marginal for FL humidity.
- **UC4A** — ground contact, general use. The right choice for most Florida deck framing.
- **UC4B** — heavy-duty ground contact. The right choice for posts and any framing that will see standing water.

**Lifespan.** 15-25 years with proper sealing every 1-3 years. 10-15 years if neglected. Florida humidity is harder on PT than drier climates.

**Cost.** $15-35 per sq ft installed (typical Central FL 2026). Lower end for simple ground-level decks; higher end for elevated decks with railings.

**Florida-specific issues.** PT pine warps, checks, and splinters as it ages. Strong solvents in some sealers can damage the surface — water-based stains are typically the better choice for FL.

> **Beit Tip:** Always specify the **UC4A or UC4B** use-category for any deck framing in contact with grade. If a contractor's quote doesn't specify the use-category, ask. The cost difference between UC3B and UC4A is small; the lifespan difference is large.

## Composite decking

Composite has become the default mid-range option for Florida homeowners who want low maintenance + long lifespan without the cost of tropical hardwoods.

**Brands available in Central FL.**

- **Trex** — Transcend, Enhance Naturals, Select, Signature lines. The market-share leader. Florida distribution is excellent.
- **TimberTech** — PRO Reserve and AZEK Vintage lines. Higher-end positioning; AZEK is fully PVC (technically not "composite") and has the best heat performance of the polymer decking options.
- **Fiberon** — Concordia, Sanctuary, Promenade lines. Solid mid-range alternative.

**Cap-stock construction.** Modern composite is "capped" — a polymer outer shell over a wood-fiber core. The cap protects against staining, fading, and mildew that plagued earlier uncapped composites.

**Lifespan.** 25-30 years typical; many brands offer 25-50 year warranties (terms vary — read carefully, especially the fade and stain warranty exclusions for chlorine pool decks).

**Cost.** $30-60 per sq ft installed (typical Central FL 2026). High end for premium lines (TimberTech AZEK Vintage, Trex Signature).

**Florida-specific issues.** **Heat.** Composite decking gets hotter than wood in direct sun — dark colors can hit 130-145°F surface temperature on a Florida summer afternoon, hot enough to burn bare feet. Light colors (driftwood gray, sand) stay 15-25°F cooler. **Mildew.** Even capped composite can grow surface mildew in shaded humid conditions; pressure-wash annually. **Weight.** Composite is heavier than wood; the framing must be sized appropriately (FBC R507 sets joist span tables — your contractor verifies).

## Cedar (the premium-look mid-range)

Western Red Cedar is sourced from Pacific Northwest mills. It's naturally rot- and insect-resistant due to its high tannin and natural oil content.

**Why cedar works in Florida (despite myths).** Cedar grew up in cool, wet Pacific Northwest climates — many homeowners assume it can't survive Florida UV. The truth is more nuanced: untreated cedar in direct Florida sun weathers to silver-gray quickly (12-24 months) and loses some surface integrity. Cedar **with proper finishing** (penetrating oil-based stain reapplied every 2-3 years) retains its honey-gold color and full structural performance for 15-25 years.

**Lifespan.** 15-25 years with biennial refinishing. 8-12 years if neglected.

**Cost.** $25-45 per sq ft installed (typical Central FL 2026).

**Aesthetic.** Cedar has a warmth that no composite product can replicate. The grain pattern and color depth are part of why high-end Florida custom homes still specify it. The trade-off is the maintenance cycle.

> **Beit Tip:** If you choose cedar in Florida, **commit to the 2-3 year refinishing cycle** before signing the contract. Skipping refinishing for 5+ years degrades cedar visibly; the warm-tone aesthetic that justified the upcost is the first thing to go. If you're not sure you'll keep up with maintenance, composite is the smarter long-term choice.

## Tropical hardwoods (ipe, cumaru, garapa)

Ipe (*Tabebuia spp.*) is the apex of natural-wood decking — extremely dense (~70 lbs/ft³), Janka hardness ~3680, naturally rot-resistant for 50+ years bare. Cumaru ("Brazilian teak") and garapa offer similar properties at slightly lower price points.

**Lifespan.** 30-50 years bare; 50+ years with annual oil application. Many tropical-hardwood decks installed in the 1980s are still in service.

**Cost.** $50-90 per sq ft installed (typical Central FL 2026). Premium pricing reflects: material rarity, harder installation (pre-drilling required because the wood breaks standard fasteners), specialized fasteners (stainless steel — galvanized rusts in FL pool/coastal environments).

**Sustainability.** Some tropical-hardwood sources have meaningful sustainability concerns. **Always specify FSC-certified material** if specifying tropical hardwood — the price premium is real but small relative to the material cost, and avoids contributing to deforestation.

**Maintenance.** Two paths: (1) annual oil to retain warm honey-brown color, or (2) let it weather naturally to silver-gray (no maintenance, no aesthetic preservation).

## Aluminum (the pool-deck specialist)

Powder-coated aluminum decking — brands like Wahoo, Versadeck, Aridek — is purpose-built for pool decks and waterfront applications.

**Lifespan.** 30+ years.

**Cost.** $40-60 per sq ft installed (typical Central FL 2026).

**Why pool decks specifically.** Aluminum doesn't degrade under chlorine, salt-water systems, or repeated wetting. It also stays cooler than composite — a major comfort advantage on a pool deck. Powder-coated finishes hold color for decades.

**Trade-off.** Modern aluminum decking has come a long way visually, but it still doesn't match the warmth of wood or the texture variety of composite. Best when "low maintenance" and "pool durability" are the top priorities.

## Decision matrix

Sort by your top priority:

| Your priority | Best pick | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lowest upfront cost | Pressure-treated pine | $15-35/sq ft. Specify UC4A or UC4B treatment. |
| Lowest maintenance | Composite (capped) | Wash 1-2x/yr. No staining, no sealing. |
| Premium look + maintenance OK | Cedar (Western Red) | $25-45/sq ft. Refinish every 2-3 years. |
| Longest lifespan + budget OK | Tropical hardwood (ipe) | $50-90/sq ft. 30-50 year service life. |
| Pool deck specifically | Aluminum or light-color composite | Aluminum stays cooler; composite is more affordable. |
| Coastal home (salt exposure) | Aluminum or PVC (TimberTech AZEK) | Salt accelerates degradation of organic materials. |
| Hurricane wind resilience | Any properly-attached system per FBC R507 | Material matters less than the framing + ledger attachment. |

For homes in [Orlando's Audubon Park / College Park](/orlando-roofing) historic neighborhoods, cedar or ipe carry the period-correct aesthetic. For newer subdivisions in [Oviedo](/oviedo-roofing) or [Winter Park](/winter-park-roofing), composite is typically the right value point.

## Maintenance basics

| Material | Annual maintenance |
| --- | --- |
| PT pine | Clean (pressure wash @ 1500 PSI max), reseal with water-based stain every 1-3 years |
| Composite | Wash with mild detergent 1-2x/year. No sealing. Inspect for mildew in shaded zones. |
| Cedar | Clean and reapply penetrating oil-based stain every 2-3 years |
| Ipe | Optional: apply ipe oil annually to preserve color. Otherwise let weather. |
| Aluminum | Occasional wash. Powder coat is durable; touch up minor scratches with manufacturer paint. |

Avoid pressure-washing composite or aluminum at high pressure — both can be marred by aggressive cleaning.

## Florida code notes

**Florida Statute Chapter 515 (Residential Swimming Pool Safety Act).** Any pool installed after 2000 requires a barrier — typically a 4-foot-tall fence with self-closing/self-latching gates. The deck design must integrate with this barrier or include alternative protection (door alarms, pool covers).

**FBC R507 (Decks).** Florida Building Code Section R507 governs deck framing, post sizing, footings, joist span tables, and ledger attachment. Two specific requirements that matter most:

- **Ledger attachment to house** must use through-bolts (not nails) for any deck more than 2 feet above grade. This is a post-2010 code change and is a frequent failure mode in older decks — the original ledger was nailed, and the deck is now structurally undersized.
- **Joist hangers** must be galvanized or stainless steel rated for the treatment chemistry of the framing. ACQ-treated wood requires specifically rated hangers (G-185 minimum) — older galvanized hangers corrode rapidly when in contact with ACQ.

A licensed General Contractor verifies these per project. Beit Building Contractors holds CGC1534077 — every deck we install meets FBC R507 with documented inspection.

> **Beit Tip:** When buying a home with an existing deck, **look at the ledger board attachment** carefully. If you see nails (not bolts) attaching the ledger to the house, the deck doesn't meet current FBC R507. It may still be safe but should be evaluated, and major remodels typically require bringing it up to current code.
