The single most common question on a Beit Building Contractors discovery call is some variant of "how long is this going to take?" The honest answer is "it depends" — but the variables are knowable, and the realistic timeline is straightforward to estimate when you understand the five phases of Florida residential construction and the specific delays that hit Central Florida projects more than anywhere else.

This guide walks through each phase, the realistic time budget, and the common delays that turn a planned 4-month project into a 7-month project. It's the framework we use when scoping any Beit project — addition, renovation, or ground-up — so you know what to expect before you sign.

> **Beit Tip:** **Pad every phase budget by 15-25%.** Florida construction has more weather-driven and permit-driven variance than most national content acknowledges. A contractor who quotes a tight timeline with no padding is either inexperienced or about to disappoint you. Honest contractors quote ranges and explain what drives the upper end.

## The 5 phases at a glance

Every Florida residential project follows roughly the same sequence — only the duration of each phase changes with project scope:

1. **Pre-construction** — design, engineering, contract, permit (3-12 weeks)
2. **Site prep + foundation** — survey, grading, slab (2-4 weeks)
3. **Framing + dried-in** — framing, roof, windows, doors (2-6 weeks)
4. **MEP + insulation** — mechanical, electrical, plumbing rough-in (2-4 weeks)
5. **Finish + occupancy** — drywall, trim, paint, fixtures, final inspections (4-8 weeks)

For a small kitchen renovation, phases compress (no foundation; lighter MEP). For a ground-up new build, every phase runs near the upper bound. We'll walk through each.

## Phase 1 — Pre-construction (3-12 weeks)

This is where most projects "lose time" before any tool is picked up. The phase splits into design + permit:

**Design + engineering (2-6 weeks).** Initial consultation → schematic design → construction documents → structural engineering review (when required by FBC 7th Edition / 2024). For straightforward additions or remodels, design can be fast. For projects that need engineered floor systems, hurricane-rated openings, or significant structural modifications, engineering review extends this phase materially.

**Contract + permit submittal (1-3 weeks).** Final scope agreement, contract signing, deposit, permit application package preparation, submittal to the appropriate county or city building department.

**Permit review (1-6 weeks, by jurisdiction).** Realistic Central Florida 2026 review timelines:

- **Orange County Building Division (unincorporated + most of Orlando metro):** 2-6 weeks for residential
- **City of Orlando Building Department:** 2-5 weeks
- **Seminole County Building Division (Oviedo, Sanford, Lake Mary):** 2-5 weeks
- **City of Winter Park Building Department:** 3-6 weeks (longer if Historic Preservation Board review is required for Park Ave / Olde Winter Park)
- **Osceola County Building Department:** 2-5 weeks

If the initial review comes back with revisions (very common for first-pass new builds), add 2-4 weeks for the design team to address comments and resubmit.

> **Beit Tip:** Permit review timeline is the single biggest variable in a Florida project. **Submit the cleanest permit package you can on the first try** — a complete package that gets approved without revisions saves 4+ weeks vs. one that comes back with comments. This is where a [licensed General Contractor with permit fluency](/blog/vet-florida-roofing-contractor) (Beit holds CGC1534077 and pulls Orange County permits weekly) makes a measurable schedule difference.

## Phase 2 — Site prep + foundation (2-4 weeks)

Once the permit is in hand, physical work starts. For renovations, this phase compresses or skips entirely. For additions and new builds:

- **Site survey + grading** — 2-4 days
- **Excavation + form work** — 3-7 days
- **Below-slab plumbing rough-in** — 2-4 days
- **Vapor barrier, rebar, termite pre-treatment** — 1-2 days (Florida termite pre-treatment is required by FBC R318)
- **Slab pour** — 1 day
- **Slab cure** — minimum 7 days before next-phase work; full structural strength at 28 days

Foundation inspections must clear before framing starts. Inspector availability typically adds 1-3 days at each inspection point.

## Phase 3 — Framing + "dried-in" (2-6 weeks)

The most visible phase from the outside — your structure goes from a slab to a recognizable building.

- **Wall framing** — 1-2 weeks for a typical addition; 2-4 weeks for a new build
- **Roof framing / truss installation** — 3-5 days
- **Roof decking + underlayment** — 2-3 days
- **Roofing material installation** — depends on material (see [Florida Roofing Materials Guide](/blog/florida-roofing-materials-guide-2026)). Architectural shingle: 1-3 days. Tile: 3-7 days. Standing-seam metal: 4-10 days.
- **Windows + exterior doors installation** — 2-4 days

The critical milestone is **"dried-in" inspection** — once the roof, windows, and exterior doors are installed, the interior is weatherproof. Subsequent work can proceed regardless of weather.

Materials lead times can extend this phase: specialty impact-rated windows (FBC HVHZ or near-coastal homes) can have 6-12 week lead times. Confirm material availability during design phase, not when framing is ready.

## Phase 4 — Mechanical, electrical, plumbing + insulation (2-4 weeks)

Inside the dried-in structure:

- **Plumbing rough-in** — 5-10 days
- **Electrical rough-in** — 5-10 days
- **HVAC rough-in (ductwork + equipment)** — 5-10 days
- **Inspections at each rough-in milestone** — Florida requires separate building, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical inspections at this phase
- **Insulation install** — 2-4 days, after MEP inspections clear

These trades typically overlap — plumbing, electrical, and HVAC crews work in different areas of the project simultaneously. The schedule risk is **inspector scheduling** — when one trade is ready for inspection but the inspector is booked 3 days out, all subsequent work stalls.

## Phase 5 — Drywall + finish + occupancy (4-8 weeks)

The longest phase for a typical project. From the outside, the building looks "done" by the start of this phase; the time is in the interior detail work:

- **Drywall hanging** — 1 week
- **Drywall finishing** (multiple coats of mud + tape, sanding) — 1-2 weeks
- **Trim carpentry** (baseboards, casings, crown if specified) — 1 week
- **Cabinetry install** — 3-5 days for a typical kitchen
- **Flooring** — 3-7 days depending on material (tile is slowest; vinyl plank is fastest)
- **Interior paint** — 1-2 weeks for whole-home; 3-5 days for renovation
- **Fixture installation** (lights, plumbing fixtures, hardware) — 2-3 days
- **Final inspections + Certificate of Occupancy** — 1-3 weeks (multiple inspections, scheduling-dependent)

Punch list (final touch-ups after walkthrough) typically takes another 3-7 days.

## Common Florida delays (the 15-25% padding)

What pushes projects past their initial estimate:

**Permit revisions.** First-submission packages frequently come back with engineering or code comments. Each revision cycle adds 2-4 weeks.

**Specialty material lead times.** FBC HVHZ-rated windows, hurricane-rated impact glass, custom-color tile, certain composite decking — all can have 4-12 week lead times. Order at design phase, not framing phase.

**Hurricane season pauses (June-November).** Outdoor framing and roofing work pauses during named-storm watches. Even unaffected weeks see daily afternoon thunderstorms (typical June-September) that halt outdoor work for 1-3 hours per afternoon. Plan critical exterior work for the dry season (December-April) when possible.

**Inspector scheduling.** Florida building departments work through inspection requests in queue. Same-day inspections are rare; 1-3 day lead times are typical. Multiple back-to-back inspections (rough-in → insulation → drywall) can compound to a full week of "waiting on inspections" across a project.

**Subcontractor scheduling.** When trades are scheduled tightly back-to-back, any delay (illness, equipment failure, prior project running long) ripples through the schedule. The contractors who hold their dates are the ones with deeper crew rosters.

> **Beit Tip:** When a contractor presents a timeline, ask explicitly: **"What's your padding for permit revisions, weather, and inspections?"** If the answer is "none, we'll hit the date," that's not realism — that's setting up for disappointment. Honest contractors budget for the unknowns.
